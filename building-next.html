The road ahead

Building next.

Tempo is built by one person, in public, around one rule: nothing is marketed before it ships. This page is the honest ledger — what's in the app today, what's being built right now, and what's planned. No dates, because dates from a one-person team are fiction. Just order.

The foundation

Shipped

Everything on the homepage is in the app now — this is the part you can hold.

  • One grid for the whole week — tasks, events, habits and focus time together, with durations that block real time.
  • A calendar built for changing weeks — a full year down to a single day, fast enough that re-entering a new fortnight isn't punishment.
  • Sharing by invitation — pair with the one person you plan with, share exactly the lists you choose, hand tasks over, and follow what changed.
  • Offline first — everything works with no connection and syncs when you're back.
  • The Grove — focus sessions that grow a forest, kept in your personal space. No leaderboards anywhere in shared spaces, ever.

Lists that reach the calendar

Shipped

A shared list is only half the job — the other half is time. The bridge is in:

  • Give any list item a date and it lands on the calendar in its list's colour, on both calendars if the list is shared.
  • Repeating list items — milk goes back on the grocery list next week by itself.
  • Whose turn it is, visible — when someone takes a task, the calendar shows who's taking it, so bin night belongs to a person, not to a list.

Commitment capture

Shipped · v0

Tempo pulls commitments out of pasted text — as proposals you approve, never silent changes. Paste an email, an announcement, a roster into “Add from Text” and Tempo lists the dated commitments it finds, on your device, for you to approve one by one. Photos and PDFs come next. The point isn't automation for its own sake: it's that the person who notices everything shouldn't have to be the one who types everything.

Capacity, not empty calendar space

Building

A two-hour gap after a long shift is not two hours of energy. The first slice shipped: every calendar day now shows how much it really holds, and moving something in the re-plan tells you what the target day already carries. Next comes the harder part — commutes, recovery, and the difference between time you have and time you can actually use.

Recovery, when the day collapses

Shipped

Every planner is designed for the day going well. Tempo asks a calm question instead of showing a wall of overdue red: "Today didn't go to plan?" One quiet review of everything hanging over today, where each thing can move (with the target day's real load beside it), shrink, be handed to the person you plan with, or be let go of on purpose. A bad day is something to recover from, not to be shamed for.

Sharing that goes further

Shipped · growing

Two rungs landed together. The privacy gradient: mark a private task “Show as Busy” and whoever you share with sees the time is taken — never what it is. And more than one person: connect with each of the people you plan with — family, a housemate, anyone — every connection its own invitation, everyone seeing only what you mark shared. (Everyone needs the current version.) Next: per-person sharing choices, so different people can see different lists.

Rules the roadmap follows

Features change; these don't.

  • Nothing changes your plan silently. Anything Tempo ever suggests — a captured commitment, a re-plan — is a proposal you approve.
  • Sharing is an invitation you accept. Never an account you're conscripted into. Both sides say yes, and either side can leave.
  • A bad day is something to recover from, not to be shamed for. No guilt-red backlogs. Recovery is a feature, not a failure state.
  • Gamification stays in your personal Grove. No points, streaks or scoreboards anywhere two people share — plans are not a competition.
  • What you pay for today never gets taken away. Early supporters keep their price for life, and your data exports free, always.
